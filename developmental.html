Developmental Theories

Piaget's Theory

Four stages of cognitive development:

  • Sensorimotor (0-2 years)
  • Preoperational (2-7 years)
  • Concrete Operational (7-11 years)
  • Formal Operational (11+ years)

Piaget's Theory of Cognitive Development

Piaget's theory explains how children's thinking and reasoning abilities develop over time. He proposed that children actively construct their understanding of the world through different stages of development.

Sensorimotor Stage (0-2 years)

During this stage, infants learn about the world through their senses and motor actions. They develop object permanence – understanding that objects continue to exist even when out of sight. Infants progress from reflexive behavior to goal-directed activities.

Preoperational Stage (2-7 years)

Children begin using symbols and language to represent objects. Their thinking is egocentric – they struggle to see things from others' perspectives. They can think about things symbolically, which is demonstrated through pretend play and drawing.

Concrete Operational Stage (7-11 years)

Children develop logical thinking about concrete situations. They understand conservation – that quantity remains the same despite changes in shape or arrangement. They can classify objects and order them in a logical sequence. Their thinking becomes less egocentric.

Formal Operational Stage (11+ years)

Individuals develop the ability to think abstractly and reason about hypothetical situations. They can engage in systematic problem-solving and understand abstract concepts in science and mathematics. They develop the capacity for complex logical reasoning.

Erikson's Theory

Eight stages of psychosocial development:

  • Trust vs. Mistrust
  • Autonomy vs. Shame
  • Initiative vs. Guilt
  • Industry vs. Inferiority

Erikson's Theory of Psychosocial Development

Erikson's theory focuses on how social experiences throughout life shape personality development. Each stage presents a conflict that must be resolved for healthy psychological development.

Trust vs. Mistrust (0-18 months)

Infants develop a sense of whether the world is safe and reliable. Consistent, responsive caregiving leads to trust, while inconsistent or neglectful care leads to mistrust. This stage forms the foundation for all future relationships.

Autonomy vs. Shame and Doubt (18 months-3 years)

Children begin asserting independence through activities like toilet training, walking, and exploring. Supportive parenting develops autonomy, while overly critical or restrictive parenting leads to self-doubt.

Initiative vs. Guilt (3-5 years)

Children take initiative in activities and play. They begin planning and undertaking tasks. Support for their initiatives builds purpose, while excessive criticism leads to guilt about their goals and desires.

Industry vs. Inferiority (5-12 years)

Children develop competence through school, peer relationships, and other activities. Success leads to a sense of competence, while failure results in feelings of inferiority.

Identity vs. Role Confusion (12-18 years)

Adolescents develop a sense of self and personal identity. They explore different roles, goals, and values. Success leads to a strong sense of personal identity, while difficulty leads to role confusion.

Intimacy vs. Isolation (18-40 years)

Young adults develop intimate relationships. Success leads to strong relationships, while failure results in loneliness and isolation.

Generativity vs. Stagnation (40-65 years)

Adults focus on career and family life. Success creates a sense of contribution to society, while failure leads to stagnation.

Ego Integrity vs. Despair (65+ years)

Older adults reflect on life. Acceptance of one's life leads to ego integrity, while regret leads to despair.

Vygotsky's Theory

Social development theory:

  • Zone of Proximal Development
  • Scaffolding
  • Social Learning
  • Cultural Context

Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory

Vygotsky's theory emphasizes how social interaction and culture shape cognitive development. He believed that learning occurs through social interaction with more knowledgeable others.

Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)

The ZPD is the difference between what a learner can do without help and what they can do with guidance from a skilled partner. This zone represents the skills and knowledge that are within reach for the learner with appropriate support.

Scaffolding

This refers to the temporary support provided by teachers, parents, or peers to help a child master a task. As the child becomes more competent, the support is gradually removed. This process helps children develop independence in their learning.

Social Learning

Learning occurs through social interaction and collaboration. Children develop higher mental functions through social activities before these functions become internalized. Language plays a crucial role in this process as both a social tool and a means for thought.

Cultural Context

Cognitive development is influenced by the cultural tools and practices of a society. These include language, number systems, memory aids, and other cultural tools that shape how children learn and think.

Role of Language

Language serves as both a means of communication and a tool for thought. Children use private speech (talking to themselves) to guide their behavior and thinking. This eventually becomes internal speech or thought.
