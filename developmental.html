Developmental Theories

Piaget's Theory

Four stages of cognitive development:

  • Sensorimotor (0-2 years)
  • Preoperational (2-7 years)
  • Concrete Operational (7-11 years)
  • Formal Operational (11+ years)

Piaget's Theory of Cognitive Development

Children actively construct their understanding of the world through distinct stages.

Sensorimotor Stage (0-2 years)

Infants learn through sensory and motor interactions. Object permanence emerges.

Preoperational Stage (2-7 years)

Symbols and language begin to develop, though thinking remains egocentric.

Concrete Operational Stage (7-11 years)

Logical thinking for concrete situations develops along with conservation skills.

Formal Operational Stage (11+ years)

Abstract reasoning and systematic problem-solving emerge, enabling advanced cognitive skills.

Erikson's Theory

Eight stages of psychosocial development:

  • Trust vs. Mistrust
  • Autonomy vs. Shame
  • Initiative vs. Guilt
  • Industry vs. Inferiority

Erikson's Theory of Psychosocial Development

This theory focuses on social experiences across the lifespan and resolving key psychosocial conflicts.

Trust vs. Mistrust (0-18 months)

Establishing trust in caregivers lays the foundation for future relationships.

Autonomy vs. Shame and Doubt (18 months-3 years)

Children develop independence while learning limits and boundaries.

Initiative vs. Guilt (3-5 years)

Encouragement in exploration fosters initiative, whereas over-criticism may lead to guilt.

Industry vs. Inferiority (5-12 years)

Productive engagement and skill mastery develop competence and self-esteem.

Vygotsky's Theory

Highlights the role of social interaction and cultural context in learning.

Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory

Learning is a collaborative process that is enhanced by guidance from more knowledgeable others.

Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)

The difference between what a child can do independently and with support.

Scaffolding

Temporary support provided to help the learner progress until they can work independently.

Social Learning

Knowledge is acquired through interactions and collaborative problem-solving.
